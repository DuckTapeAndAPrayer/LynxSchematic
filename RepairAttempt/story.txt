Who am I:
     I am a friend of #28260 Coral Tech FTC team and I help them with ideas and
technical knowledge. I work in IT industry, over the years I did and still do software 
development for embedded devices, networking, telemetry, digital forensics and digital research.
I also have a passion to understant how things works and digging into electronic devices
was a hobby since I was in elementary school. I have no official role on the team and I
explicitly requested not to be included as a mentor, but I assured them I will be here 
supporting them everytime they need. We were together when this story begun...

     How I got into this:
     The team built a robot for 2025/2026 FTC decode season and had only 2 and a half batteries
for the robot. I say 2 and a half because one of those is at least 4 years old, 
it fully charges in under 10 minutes at 1A and it lasts almost 20 seconds of operation 
until hub complains about low voltage. For those of you who built a robot and compete
in FTC, you may know how many functional tests you do, how many code uploads and how
many thousands of autonomy scenarios and adjustments you make.
     So, back to our 2 batteries: to avoid changing and charging those, we thought it will
be better to power our robot from a power supply when we program and test it. Don't think
we powered it from a chinese power supply or worse, directly from a battery charger. We
found a stabilised lab power supply with adjustable voltage and current limiter. It worked
perfect, but a drawback was that it can supply only 5A, not enough if you want to move
the robot. Solution? A 6-pack of 2.7V/500F supercaps. This solved the problem of instant
power delivery to motors: when the power supply source cannot delivery all the power, energy
stored in capacitors provided the extra amount then, when you stop moving, they are recharged.
It worked flawlessly for months.
     In the fatal day, we just pressed the official rev switch to turn on the robot like we
did for hundreds of time before. I always look at the hub when I turn it on to see if the led
lights blue to confirm the power on. It didn't turn on this time and I think I heard a 
hissing sound (it wasn't perfectly quiet, there were people around moving and talking). I turn
it off in about 2 seconds because something didn't look right. I looked again at the wires 
to check if we connected something wrong, if the isolation is not broken and other issues.
Nothing was wrong, so we turn it back on again. Led stay off, expansion hub started, servo 
power module started but main hub refused to start. This was the moment when we saw a little
smoke coming out of the hub.
     First question on our lips was: what the #^(! just happened? (this word was removed
because it breaks the FIRST Gracious Professionalism code). We saw this happen to another
team, during a competition, no more than 3 weeks ago and they were forced to abandon 
and were disqualified. In a few minutes it was clear that some catastrophyc failure
happened inside the hub and it is dead. We are living in the EU, the Rev is in US, we have
regionals in 2 weeks and no spare control hub. Only the Rev Robotics can officialy repair a 
broken control hub, so it was clear that solution will not work for us. Next day we ordered
a new control hub from the EU supplier (it came in 3 days) but meanwhile we agreed to open
the broken one to try to repair it. We assumed that some buck converter IC exploded because
they are known to be prone to fail on startup due to high inrush current or to high transient
voltage generated by the bounce of a mechanical switch.
     The buck converter can fail in 2 modes: one civilised way when it explodes and the story
ends here and one catastrophic, when input voltage flows into the regulated voltage domain.
We removed the exploded TPS562209 and we measured resistance on the terminals of the C63.
We found it to be something about 1 ohm, so the conclusion was clear: a short between power
and ground exists in 3v3 domain, which is anything but a good news. We took our lab power source, 
set it to 3v3 150mA and powered the capacitor: voltage dropped under 1V and the power source 
entered in current limit mode. Then we slowly increased the current to about 700mA to check 
what is heating up and we found to be the main microcontroller of the board, the TM4C123HP6.
We removed it from the board and the short circuit dissapeared, then the remaining components
of the board draw aprox 180mA at 3.3V and nothing is heating any more.
     The conclusion is now clear: 12V rushed into 3v3 domain and we assume everything powered
from that rail is broken, with high chances to break also the 5v domain (servo controllers),
wich we didn't yet checked. Unfortunately, on 3v3 rail there are connected all the ICs like usb
bridge, IMU, and rs485 transceiver. Next quick test was to connect the board to a computer
through mini-usb port, to see it the usb bridge is still alive. If it was, then something
like FT232 serial converter should have appear in device manager, but nothing did.
     In all the mess, a good news: android board is still working. At least, it boots up, 
hdmi is working but it says it cannot communicate with internal expansion hub. We will see in
the future if the part which is actually linked to lynx board is still working or the burst of
12V also damaged something in the android board.
     So far, we found that 3v3 buck is dead (exploded), main processor is dead (heating up
at 1V), and usb bridge FT230XQ is also dead. We have all the reasons to believe that the IMU
is also dead, the RS485 transceiver and virtually anything which have and absolute maximum rating
below 12v, including diodes, transistors and maybe some small value resistors.
     We came to the theory that it was the perfect condition generated by switch bounce combined
with wire lenghts and external capacitors which created at least a spike in input voltage 
exceeding 17V. After this treshold, upside mosfet inside TPS562209 failed, leaving unregulated
voltage to flow into 3v3 bus. Another theory is that TPS562209 encountered an unexpected
condition when it tried to start under an unstable input voltage generated by switch bounce and opened
both upside and downside power mos transistors at the same time, causing one of them to fail.
In either case, in milliseconds, everything failed and TPS562209 exploded. 
     This situation could have been prevented by the designers of the lynx board by adding a TVS diode
before the buck, adding a tvs diode after the buck or by simply not relying on TPS562209's startup
delay of 1ms by adding a RC bridge on EN pin to allow a greater startup time, knowing the fact
that theirs board will be used in conditions where a certain mechanical switch is required
between the battery and the board. This chip is starting under heavy load due to all components
connected on 3v3 domain, including the small capacitors scattered all over the board which are
at 0V and all demand a high current in fist milliseconds after power on. As you may already
guessed so far, the external 20A fuse from the power line still survived after this event :)
     What is different between a pack of supercaps and a battery is the ESR or internal resistance.
One supercap have an ESR of about 5 mOhms, so a pack of six should have about 25 mOhms. A NiMH 
battery cell is around 30-50 mOhms so a pack of 10-series cells should be 300-500mOhm. This
would limit the inrush current to only 24A (in theory) instead 480A provided by capacitors, either
case being way to more too big to prevent 3v3 buck failure. This is why I think the capacitors
were not the cause of the disaster, but they were a favorable factor. Since we saw a hub failing
the same way in an official competition, using official gobilda battery, we are inclined to
believe this is a design error for an edge-case event rather than improper use.

     The plan for the future is something like this:
0. Check transistors, diodes and resistors on the board with a multimeter to see any abnormal values
1. replace the main microcontroller TM4C123HP6 and add JTAG connector to the board
2. try to upload firmware into it with JTAG (power the board using limited current, using lab source)
3. check if is booting up, check if it say something on serial debug interface(s)
4. connect the android board to check if the communication between boards is still working
5. Add 12V power to the board, check if android is still alive using supplied power from lynx
6. Check if 5v domain is still working
7. Check RS485 and USB, replace the IC if necessary (I strongly believe it will be)
8. Add a new IMU board, connected externally to I2C bus 0, because this board uses a BGA IMU and
       I am not confident I will be able to resolder it in place.
9. add 3v3 buck converter, TPS562209, to verify that whole hub runs under its own power
